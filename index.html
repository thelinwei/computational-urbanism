From Data Gaps
to Human Holding

The research asks how computational methods can help identify threshold spaces that support human relational life. The work uses data to locate where deeper human inquiry should begin.

The challenge
Urban analytics increasingly infers public life from the street: street-view imagery, road-facing sensing, mapped amenities. In a dense tropical city, much of that life is sheltered, interiorised, and pedestrian-scaled — void decks, linkways, hawker rims, playground edges. The question is not whether these spaces matter. It is whether the instruments used to model the city can see them at all.
The contribution
Three open-data pilots on Singapore's covered pedestrian network establish that the blind spot is real, non-random, and structured.

The claim
Road-based urban sensing is not blind at random. It is blind in a way that correlates with where the city has built the conditions for public life. That correlation is a sampling frame: it says where fieldwork on relational holding should look first, and it is what the doctoral study is designed to test.

The human concern

The research begins from a regenerative urbanist concern: cities should not only optimise movement, density, comfort, or resource flows. They should also renew the everyday conditions for co-presence, weak-tie recognition, informal care, intergenerational overlap, and low-pressure belonging. These conditions often appear in ordinary semi-public thresholds rather than iconic civic spaces.

The data problem

Many dominant urban-analytics methods depend on road-based visibility: street-view imagery, road-facing sensing, formal amenities, and mapped public-space categories. Yet in tropical high-density cities, much social life is interiorised, sheltered, and pedestrian-scaled. The research therefore asks what kinds of relational urban environments become under-seen when the city is modelled from the road.

The methodological stance

The open-data pilots are not presented as final evidence of lived experience. They are presented as attention-directing instruments: reproducible ways to detect where spatial capacity, data invisibility, and later human observation may productively meet. The aim is to move from measurable spatial preconditions toward fieldwork, situated accounts, and design implications.

The research arc

The work therefore proceeds in tiers: first, detect what urban data can and cannot see; second, identify where lingering-supportive affordances are present; third, model whether those affordances form connected fields; fourth, validate whether those fields correspond to observed and reported relational holding.

Section 02+

Research Direction: From Affordance to Field

Threshold affordance is the local condition. Threshold transmissibility is the networked condition. Relational holding is the human condition that later fieldwork must test. The three are dependent: each layer supplies the terms the next one operates on.

LayerWhat it establishesWhere the claim ends
Threshold affordanceSpatial capacity. A segment is near infrastructure that materially supports lingering.Capacity, not occurrence. Observed use requires fieldwork.
Threshold transmissibilityNetworked capacity. Affordance potential may propagate through connected covered pedestrian spaces.Structure, not sociality. Trust and care are not measured here.
Relational holdingHuman meaning. A space may support co-presence, recognition, accompaniment, and low-stakes encounter.Established through observation and situated accounts, not open data.
Regenerative urbanismDesign implication. Cities regenerate social tissue through repeated everyday holding conditions.Holds only where tied to the evidence above.
Plate 01 · graph-diffused affordance exposure
$$X_i^{(k+1)} = (1-\textcolor{#27e0c4}{\rho})\,\textcolor{#89c7ff}{A_i} \;+\; \textcolor{#27e0c4}{\rho} \sum_j W_{ij}\,\textcolor{#89c7ff}{X_j^{(k)}}$$
How to read it: each threshold segment begins with its own local affordance score A. Part of that value remains local; part diffuses through connected neighbouring segments. ρ is the transmissibility parameter. W is the graph connection strength. The output is not a human-experience score; it is a map of where affordance potential becomes spatially networked.

Margin note: “spatial contagion” remains a useful private shorthand, lightly inspired by Erdős and the mathematics of connectivity. The formal research language is threshold transmissibility, graph-diffused affordance exposure, and relational affordance fields.

Section 03+

Open-Data Pilots as Evidence

Three pilots run in sequence on Singapore's covered pedestrian network, each taking the previous one's output as its input. Together they establish that road-based urban sensing has a systematic blind spot, that the blind spot coincides with lingering-supportive infrastructure, and that it propagates along the pedestrian network's own topology rather than along simple distance.

Pilot 01 · Visibility
Seeing the Covered City
Singapore's covered pedestrian network is cross-referenced against drivable-road proximity to estimate which segments are plausibly visible or invisible to road-based street-view imagery.
Current result:
26.5% of the covered network — approximately 1,567 of 5,911 km — is plausibly beyond road-based camera reach.
Open pilot repository →
Pilot 02 · Affordance
Where the Invisible City Could Afford to Linger
SVI-invisible and SVI-visible covered segments are compared by proximity to benches, shelters, hawker/food courts, community centres, and playgrounds.
Current result:
19.5% of SVI-invisible segments are affordance-adjacent at 20 m, compared with 6.1% of SVI-visible segments.
Open pilot repository →
Pilot 03 · Transmissibility
Does the Network Carry the Signal?
A first, narrower validation step ahead of the full graph-diffusion model (Plate 01): does SVI-invisibility itself cluster more along the covered network's own connectivity than along simple geographic proximity?
Current result:
Moran's I rises from 0.64 (simple proximity, KNN-6) to 0.79 (walkway-connectivity graph) for SVI-visibility — a +0.15 gap, p<0.001. Invisibility clusters more tightly along the network than along distance alone.
Open pilot repository →
The research does not claim that open data can see human relations. It asks whether open data can reveal where the city has built the spatial preconditions for such relations — and where dominant sensing systems fail to register them. Positioning line for applications

Margin note: this test operates directly on SVI-visibility, not yet on a diffused affordance score. It is evidence that the network-connectivity logic behind Plate 01 already holds before the fuller diffusion equation is run on a real affordance dataset — a validated stepping stone, not a substitute for it.
