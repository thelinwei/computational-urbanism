# Method notes — the four-tier research logic

The research proceeds in tiers. Each tier has a different evidentiary burden,
and the strongest version of this work is explicit about which tier a given
claim belongs to, rather than letting a Tier 1 result quietly read as a
Tier 4 one.

| Tier | Question | What it can claim | What it must not overclaim | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **01 — Data visibility** | Which pedestrian threshold spaces are visible or invisible to road-based urban sensing? | A reproducible visibility/invisibility classification of the covered network | That invisibility itself means anything socially — it's a data-quality finding first | Done (Pilot 01) |
| **02 — Threshold affordance** | Which spaces are materially equipped for pausing, waiting, sitting, eating, playing, gathering? | Spatial capacity — a segment is near infrastructure that materially supports lingering | It cannot prove anyone actually lingers there | Done (Pilot 02) |
| **03 — Threshold transmissibility** | Do local affordances (or the visibility signal that stands in for them) remain isolated, or form connected fields across the pedestrian network? | Networked capacity — affordance/visibility potential may propagate through connected covered spaces | It cannot prove trust, care, or social comfort spreads | Partially done — Pilot 03 validates the network-vs-proximity logic on SVI-visibility; the full graph-diffusion model (Plate 01) on an affordance variable is not yet run |
| **04 — Relational holding** | Do candidate fields actually hold people through lingering, co-presence, care, recognition, repeated encounter? | Human meaning — validated through observation and situated accounts | Must not become a vague positive label detached from evidence | Not yet started — future fieldwork phase |

## Why the tiers are ordered this way

Tier 1 is a data-quality argument before it's anything else: some of what
urban analytics treats as "no data" is really "no *road-based* data" —
segments that are covered, interiorised, pedestrian-scaled don't register
the same way to road-facing sensing systems. That's worth establishing on
its own before asking whether the invisible parts matter.

Tier 2 asks whether the invisible parts are systematically different, or just
randomly distributed — i.e. whether invisibility correlates with anything
socially interesting (proximity to lingering-supportive infrastructure) or
is simply noise.

Tier 3 asks a structural question about Tier 1/2's findings: is the pattern
better explained by the network's own topology than by simple distance? This
is the open-data ceiling — the most structural claim that can be made without
observing actual human behaviour.

Tier 4 is the only tier that can speak to relational holding directly, and it
requires fieldwork. Tiers 1–3 exist to make that fieldwork's site selection
principled rather than anecdotal — see the sampling-frame quadrant on the
landing page (Section 05) for how a mismatch between what the model expects
and what observation later reveals becomes the most valuable kind of site.

## The residual, across tiers

A recurring move in this work: treat disagreement between what a model
predicts and what's actually observed as the interesting finding, not noise
to average away. Pilot 03's LISA spatial outliers (HL/LH clusters) are one
instance of this — segments whose visibility status runs against their
network neighbourhood are exactly the sites a future fieldwork phase should
prioritise, because that's where the open-data model's assumptions and the
lived reality of the space diverge.
